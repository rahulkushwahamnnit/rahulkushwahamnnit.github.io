Making Websites is an Art Form

In Culture by Union

An Event Apart you say? Didn’t you guys just blog about it last week? We’re glad you noticed! Check out John’s post if you haven’t already. (Side note: have you seen his autographed swag?)

Now it’s my turn to take the hot seat. I’ll try to keep it short, and thus will focus on two of the topics that are particularly challenging me at the moment.

Content is King

My understanding of design has always been that it’s the (hopefully) intriguing relationship of form and function. There is always some purpose or end goal to its existence, and the form it takes either adds to or detracts from that purpose. We’re all aware of the notion that form follows function, but how often do we as designers remember that it’s really the function that comes first? With so many tools at our fingertips, it’s easy to get distracted by the shiny object in the room and forget your primary purpose.

This doesn’t mean it has to be dry and boring—au contraire! Creativity and personality in your work enhances the absorption of the content, increases memorability, and shows you really care about what you’re talking about. And they are especially effective when appropriate to the content. Like the great Milton Glaser says, “The purpose of [design] is to inform and delight.” Well, he says art, but art and design are sometimes one and the same.

A few “rules”:

 

  • Remove as many distractions as possible, (or K.I.S.S., Keep it simple, stupid).
  • Make the same content available on desktop and mobile, even if it looks different in various applications
  • Tell a unique story with your website/digital experience. What makes you who you are?

 

Be a craftsman

So what does caring about your content look like? Aarron Walter, the Director of UX at Mailchimp, shared several examples of the many ways you can honor your content. You can be witty, cheesy, elegant, etc., all depending on your topic. However you choose to approach your content, you must craft it. The extra mile or two or ten you go reinforces that your content matters to you and it should matter to your user. Plus, says Walter, it’s these added, seemingly inconsequential details that afford you an extra measure of grace by your customers when you might fall short. They understand that you care about their experience of your company, so they’ll most likely be willing to stick with you if you happen to goof up.

See craft in action here:

 

  • Voice and Tone
  • Noble Denim
  • David A. Smith (I don’t care if you like John Mayer’s music or not, this level of craft is so rare).

 

Craft does not necessarily mean your website is the most technologically advanced and chock-full of bells and whistles. As designers, it means paying attention to the details so that readability and accessibility are optimized. For example, pay close attention to your typography—how it renders in a pixel-based world and what features does the type offer? (Check out these CSS3/type tricks). Though typography and html are not a match made in heaven, they can still play nice.

Remember: a crafted website is one that begins with a deep understanding of its content, and lets the content direct the conversation. There is beauty in simplicity. Your best work as a designer is done when the story you’re telling is compelling—enhanced by its form—and meaningful to your user.
